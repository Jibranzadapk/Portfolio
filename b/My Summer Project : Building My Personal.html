🌱 My Summer Project: Building My Personal Portfolio Website.

When my first semester ended, I had a lot of free time during the summer break. While most people think of summer vacation as a chance to relax, for me it felt like the perfect time to try something new and actually build something that would last. I wanted to create a project that not only improved my skills but also became a part of my personal growth journey. That’s when I decided to build my own personal portfolio website from scratch.

I still remember the excitement of opening my editor, starting with a blank HTML file, and thinking: “This is going to be my space on the internet.”

Why I Decided to Build It

As a student, it’s easy to just go with classes, assignments, and exams without really creating something for yourself. But I didn’t want to wait until the end of my degree to show what I can do. I wanted to make something early — something that represented me, my projects, my growth, and even my thoughts.

Also, there’s something special about having your own corner on the internet. A personal
website feels like your digital home — where people can come in, learn about you, and see what you’ve been working on.

Starting Out: Clean, Simple, and “Hacker Vibes”

I wanted my website to feel unique, not like another template-based portfolio. That’s why I went with a green-on-black theme — kind of like hacker vibes. It reminded me of those classic terminal screens, but I made sure to keep it clean and modern.

The idea was simple:

  • Green text, black background
  • Minimal clutter
  • A smooth, futuristic feel

It wasn’t just about design; it also represented the way I see myself: someone who loves
simplicity but also enjoys a touch of creativity.

Building Step by Step

Since this was my first big personal web project, I kept things simple but focused on learning. I didn’t use any fancy frameworks — just HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Every line of code was written by me.

I set up a GitHub repo from the start so I could track my progress and push updates as I went. This was a game-changer because it helped me practice using Git properly and also gave me a nice timeline of my journey.

The site structure grew slowly:

  • Home Page – A hero section with a bold intro and a welcoming vibe.
  • About Page – More detailed personal information, my story, and what I’m learning.
  • Projects Page – A card-style layout where I can upload and showcase my projects.
  • Blogs Page – A section where I can share my thoughts, experiences, and writing.
  • Contact Page – With a working form that directly sends messages to my email, plussocial links.

I even made sure everything was responsive, so it works across devices. I didn’t want it to look broken on mobile, and testing it on my phone felt super rewarding.

Making It Feel Dynamic

Even though it’s technically a static website, I didn’t want it to feel lifeless. That’s why I added features that make it feel more alive:

  • A blog section where I can keep posting new writings as I grow.
  • A projects section where I can update my work over time.
  • Some interactive scripts in JavaScript that add small effects and details.

This way, the website is always a “living project.” It grows as I grow.

Features

Here are some highlights of the site:

  1. Clean UI – Everything looks neat and organized. No messy design.
  2. Green-on-black theme – A unique style that feels hacker-ish but modern.
  3. Responsive design – Works across desktops, tablets, and mobiles.
  4. Contact form – Not just a dummy form — it actually sends data to my email.
  5. Projects & Blogs – Easy to update and expand as I move forward.
  6. Cross-platform optimization – Tested to look good everywhere.

It might sound simple, but for me, each feature was an achievement because I built it all myself.

Beyond Just Code

Of course, it wasn’t all technical. The project also had a personal side:

  • Discipline: Working on the site during summer required me to stay consistent.
  • Creativity: Thinking about design choices and user experience pushed me to be morecreative.
  • Ownership: This wasn’t an assignment or group work — it was mine. Every success andmistake was my responsibility.

It gave me a sense of ownership that I hadn’t felt before with school projects.

Final Thoughts

When I started this project, I just wanted to fill my summer with something useful. But what I ended up creating is much more than just a static site. It’s a reflection of me — my skills, my creativity, and my journey as a student who wants to grow.

Having a personal portfolio website might seem small, but for me, it’s huge. It’s proof that with some time, focus, and curiosity, you can build something meaningful.

And the best part? This is just the beginning.

So yeah, this was my summer project after my first semester — building jibranzada.com. It’s not perfect, but it’s mine. And I can’t wait to keep making it better.
